  quiz:
  - q: What is the first step of the scientific method?
    a: Observation / Ask a question.
  - q: What is a hypothesis?
    a: A testable prediction based on evidence or prior knowledge.
  - q: Name the three types of variable.
    a: Independent variable (IV), Dependent variable (DV), Controlled variable (CV).
  - q: What is the IV?
    a: The variable the scientist deliberately changes.
  - q: What is the DV?
    a: The variable that is measured — it depends on the IV.
  - q: What makes a test 'fair'?
    a: Only ONE variable changes at a time; all others are kept the same.
  - q: What is the SI unit for length?
    a: Metre (m).
  - q: What is the SI unit for mass?
    a: Kilogram (kg).
  - q: What is the SI unit for temperature?
    a: Degrees Celsius (°C) or Kelvin (K).
  - q: When should you use a bar chart?
    a: For categorical (discrete) data — e.g. favourite colours, types of rock.
  - q: When should you use a line graph?
    a: For continuous data that changes over time.
  - q: What is the difference between qualitative and quantitative data?
    a: Qualitative = words (descriptions). Quantitative = numbers (measurements).
  - q: What is the difference between an observation and an inference?
    a: Observation = what you see/measure (fact). Inference = a conclusion drawn from observations.
  - q: Name THREE pieces of lab safety equipment.
    a: Safety goggles, lab coat, gloves, fire extinguisher, eye wash station.
  - q: Why is it important to repeat an experiment multiple times?
    a: Repeating improves reliability. One result could be a fluke — multiple trials show if the pattern is consistent.
  - q: A student changes TWO variables at once. Why is this a problem?
    a: You can't tell which variable caused the change. A fair test changes only ONE variable so you know what caused any difference.
  - q: What is the difference between accuracy and reliability?
    a: Accuracy = how close to the true value. Reliability = how consistent results are when repeated. You can be reliable but inaccurate.

  quiz:
  - q: What does the particle model say about matter?
    a: All matter is made of tiny particles that are always moving.
  - q: Describe particle arrangement in a solid.
    a: Close together in a regular, fixed pattern. Particles vibrate in place.
  - q: Describe particle arrangement in a liquid.
    a: Close together but able to slide over each other. No fixed pattern.
  - q: Describe particle arrangement in a gas.
    a: Far apart, moving quickly in random directions. Lots of empty space.
  - q: What is melting?
    a: Solid → Liquid. Particles gain energy and break free of their fixed positions.
  - q: What is condensation?
    a: Gas → Liquid. Particles lose energy and move closer together.
  - q: What is sublimation? Give an example.
    a: 'Solid → Gas directly (skipping liquid). Example: dry ice (solid CO₂).'
  - q: What happens to particle energy when you heat a substance?
    a: Particles gain kinetic energy and move faster.
  - q: What is a mixture?
    a: Two or more substances physically combined but not chemically joined.
  - q: In a solution, what is the solute and what is the solvent?
    a: Solute = the substance that dissolves. Solvent = the liquid it dissolves in.
  - q: How does filtration work?
    a: Filter paper traps insoluble solid particles while the liquid passes through.
  - q: How does evaporation recover a dissolved solid?
    a: Heating the solution evaporates the water, leaving the solid behind.
  - q: What is chromatography used for?
    a: Separating mixtures of dyes or inks — different colours travel different distances on paper.
  - q: What is a saturated solution?
    a: A solution where no more solute can dissolve at that temperature.
  - q: 'Name the change of state: Liquid → Solid'
    a: Freezing.
  - q: 'Common mistake: ''Particles expand when heated.'' Why is this wrong?'
    a: Particles DON'T get bigger. They move faster and spread further apart. The GAPS between particles increase, not the particles themselves.
  - q: Why does chocolate melt in your hand?
    a: Your body heat (37°C) is above chocolate's melting point (~34°C). Heat energy transfers to the chocolate, making particles vibrate faster until they break free of their fixed positions.
  - q: Explain why you can smell perfume from across a room.
    a: Diffusion — perfume particles spread out from high concentration to low concentration through the air. Gas particles move randomly in all directions.

  quiz:
  - q: Name the four layers of the Earth from the outside in.
    a: Crust → Mantle → Outer Core → Inner Core
  - q: Which layer is the thickest?
    a: The mantle (~2,900 km thick).
  - q: Why is the inner core solid even though it is the hottest layer?
    a: Extreme pressure from all the layers above keeps it solid.
  - q: What is the difference between oceanic and continental crust?
    a: Oceanic crust is thin (5–10 km) and dense. Continental crust is thick (30–70 km) and less dense.
  - q: What causes convection currents in the mantle?
    a: Heat from the core (radioactive decay). Hot rock rises, cools, sinks, and the cycle repeats.
  - q: What is the mechanism that moves tectonic plates?
    a: Convection currents in the mantle.
  - q: Name the three types of plate boundary.
    a: Divergent (apart), Convergent (together), Transform (sideways).
  - q: What happens at a divergent boundary?
    a: Plates move apart. Magma rises to fill the gap, creating new crust (sea-floor spreading).
  - q: What is subduction?
    a: When a denser oceanic plate sinks beneath another plate at a convergent boundary.
  - q: What was Pangaea?
    a: A supercontinent ~300 million years ago when all land was joined together.
  - q: Name TWO pieces of evidence Wegener used for continental drift.
    a: Matching fossils across continents, coastline shapes fit together, matching rock types, tropical fossils in cold regions.
  - q: Why was Wegener's theory rejected at first?
    a: He had no mechanism — he couldn't explain HOW the continents moved.
  - q: What is the Ring of Fire?
    a: A zone around the Pacific Ocean with ~75% of the world's volcanoes and ~90% of earthquakes.
  - q: 'Complete the chain: Core heat → _____ → _____ → surface features'
    a: Core heat → convection currents → plate movement → surface features.
  - q: Which boundary type does NOT produce volcanoes?
    a: Transform boundaries — they only produce earthquakes.
  - q: If tectonic plates move so slowly, why do earthquakes happen suddenly?
    a: Plates push against each other for years, building up pressure. When the friction is overcome, they slip suddenly — releasing all that stored energy as an earthquake.
  - q: 'Common mistake: ''The mantle is liquid.'' Why is this misleading?'
    a: The mantle is mostly SOLID rock, but it flows very slowly (like thick honey) due to extreme heat and pressure. It's semi-solid, not liquid.
  - q: Why are earthquakes and volcanoes found in the same places?
    a: Both happen at plate boundaries where plates interact. The same forces that cause plates to collide (volcanoes) also cause them to slip (earthquakes).

  - q: Give an example of a behavioural adaptation.

    a: The variety of different species in an area.
  - q: Why is high biodiversity important?

  - q: Name THREE human activities that harm ecosystems.
    a: Deforestation, pollution (air/water/land), overfishing, burning fossil fuels.

  - q: What happens if a predator is removed from a food web?
    a: Prey population increases → they overeat their food source → food source declines → knock-on effects.
  - q: If all the decomposers disappeared, what would happen to an ecosystem?
    a: Dead organisms would pile up. Nutrients would be locked in dead matter and never recycled back to the soil. Plants would eventually run out of nutrients and die.
  - q: Why do food webs give a more accurate picture than food chains?
    a: Most organisms eat multiple things and are eaten by multiple predators. A chain shows just one path; a web shows the real complexity of feeding relationships.

    a: Earth's 23.5° tilt means when the Northern Hemisphere tilts AWAY from the Sun (winter), the Southern Hemisphere tilts TOWARDS it (summer). Opposite seasons.
  - q: Why do we always see the same side of the Moon?
    a: The Moon rotates on its axis in exactly the same time it takes to orbit Earth (~27 days). This is called tidal locking.
  - q: 'Common mistake: ''Seasons are caused by Earth being closer to the Sun.'' Why is this wrong?'
    a: Earth's distance from the Sun barely changes. Seasons are caused by the TILT of Earth's axis (23.5°), which changes the angle and duration of sunlight.

  quiz:
  - q: What is Locard's Exchange Principle?
    a: '''Every contact leaves a trace.'' When two objects touch, material is always transferred between them.'
  - q: Name the three main fingerprint patterns.
    a: Loop (most common ~60%), whorl (~35%), arch (~5%).
  - q: Why must a crime scene be sealed off?
    a: To prevent contamination — any new material introduced could destroy or alter evidence.
  - q: How does chromatography separate inks?
    a: Different dyes have different solubilities, so they travel at different speeds through the paper, separating into distinct spots.
  - q: A ransom note was written in black pen. How could forensic scientists identify which pen was used?
    a: Run chromatography on the note ink and compare the pattern to known pen samples. Matching patterns = same ink.
  - q: What can fibre evidence tell investigators?
    a: It can link a suspect to a location by matching fibres found at the scene to their clothing.
  - q: Why are gloves worn at crime scenes?
    a: To prevent contamination — the investigator's own fingerprints, DNA, or fibres could compromise evidence.
  - q: 'Common mistake: ''DNA evidence and fingerprint evidence are the same.'' Explain the difference.'
    a: Fingerprints are physical ridge patterns on skin. DNA is genetic material inside cells. Both are unique identifiers but are completely different types of evidence.
  - q: What is a control sample and why is it important?
    a: A known sample for comparison. Without it, you can't confirm whether an unknown sample matches.
  - q: Explain why forensic science must follow the scientific method.
    a: Evidence must be collected systematically, tested fairly, and conclusions based on evidence — otherwise it wouldn't hold up in court.

  quiz:
  - q: Name three organelles found in BOTH animal and plant cells.
    a: Nucleus, cell membrane, cytoplasm, mitochondria, ribosomes.
  - q: Name three structures found ONLY in plant cells.
    a: Cell wall, chloroplasts, large permanent vacuole.
  - q: What is the function of mitochondria?
    a: Release energy from glucose through aerobic respiration. The 'powerhouses' of the cell.
  - q: An eyepiece is ×10, objective is ×40. What is the total magnification?
    a: ×400 — eyepiece × objective = 10 × 40 = 400.
  - q: What is diffusion?
    a: The net movement of particles from an area of HIGH concentration to an area of LOW concentration.
  - q: Give an example of diffusion in the human body.
    a: Oxygen diffuses from the lungs (high O₂) into the blood (low O₂).
  - q: Why are red blood cells shaped like discs?
    a: Large surface area to volume ratio for maximum oxygen absorption. No nucleus = more room for haemoglobin.
  - q: Why do root hair cells have a long extension?
    a: Increases surface area for absorbing water and minerals from the soil.
  - q: 'Common mistake: ''Plant cells don''t have mitochondria because they have chloroplasts.'' Why is this wrong?'
    a: Plants need BOTH. Chloroplasts make glucose (photosynthesis, only in light). Mitochondria release energy from glucose (respiration, 24/7).
  - q: What would happen if you removed the cell membrane?
    a: The cell would die — it couldn't control what enters and leaves. Toxic substances could flood in and essential molecules would leak out.
  - q: Explain why diffusion is faster at higher temperatures.
    a: Higher temperature = particles have more kinetic energy = they move faster = they collide and spread more quickly.
  - q: What is the difference between a cell and a tissue?
    a: A cell is the basic unit. A tissue is a group of similar cells working together for one function (e.g. muscle tissue).

  quiz:
  - q: What is a force?
    a: A push or pull that can change an object's speed, direction, or shape. Measured in Newtons (N).
  - q: Name three types of force.
    a: Gravity, friction, air resistance, tension, normal (contact/reaction), magnetic, electrostatic.
  - q: What happens when forces are balanced?
    a: Resultant force = 0. The object stays still or continues at constant speed (Newton's 1st Law).
  - q: A car has 5000N driving force and 3000N friction. What is the resultant force?
    a: 5000 − 3000 = 2000N forward. The car accelerates.
  - q: Speed = distance ÷ time. A cyclist travels 30 km in 1.5 hours. Find the speed.
    a: 30 ÷ 1.5 = 20 km/h.
  - q: What is pressure?
    a: 'Force per unit area. P = F ÷ A. Units: Pascals (Pa).'
  - q: A 900N force on 3m² area. What is the pressure?
    a: P = 900 ÷ 3 = 300 Pa.
  - q: Why do sharp knives cut more easily?
    a: 'Smaller area → higher pressure for the same force. P = F/A: less area = more pressure.'
  - q: What is the difference between mass and weight?
    a: Mass = amount of matter (kg), constant everywhere. Weight = force of gravity (N), changes with gravitational field strength.
  - q: 'Calculate weight: mass 60 kg, g = 10 N/kg.'
    a: W = mg = 60 × 10 = 600 N.
  - q: 'Common mistake: ''A moving object with no forces on it will slow down.'' Why is this wrong?'
    a: 'Newton''s 1st Law: an object in motion stays in motion unless acted on by a force. With no forces, it moves at constant speed forever.'
  - q: Why does a falling skydiver eventually reach terminal velocity?
    a: Air resistance increases with speed until it equals weight. Forces become balanced → constant speed (no acceleration).

  quiz:
  - q: What is the difference between a mixture and a compound?
    a: 'Mixture: substances not chemically joined, can be separated physically. Compound: elements chemically bonded, needs a chemical reaction to separate.'
  - q: How would you separate sand from salt water?
    a: 'Step 1: Filter to remove sand (insoluble). Step 2: Evaporate the filtrate to get salt crystals.'
  - q: When would you use distillation instead of evaporation?
    a: When you want to KEEP the liquid (solvent). Evaporation loses the liquid; distillation collects it by condensing the vapour.
  - q: How can you tell if a substance is pure using its boiling point?
    a: A pure substance has a fixed, sharp boiling point. An impure substance boils over a range of temperatures.
  - q: Why does ink separate in chromatography?
    a: Different dyes have different solubilities in the solvent, so they travel at different speeds and separate into distinct spots.
  - q: 'Common mistake: ''Filtration can separate salt from water.'' Why is this wrong?'
    a: Salt is DISSOLVED (soluble) — it passes through filter paper with the water. You need evaporation or distillation for dissolved substances.
  - q: Give a real-world use of distillation.
    a: Desalination (making drinking water from sea water), producing spirits/alcohol, purifying chemicals in labs.
  - q: What is the Rf value in chromatography?
    a: Rf = distance moved by substance ÷ distance moved by solvent. Used to identify unknown substances by comparing to known Rf values.
  - q: Explain why impure water has a higher boiling point than pure water.
    a: Dissolved impurities disrupt the structure, requiring more energy to break the bonds between water molecules.
  - q: A student filters muddy water and says it's now pure. Are they correct?
    a: No — the water may look clear but still contain dissolved substances. Pure water boils at exactly 100°C; this water likely doesn't.

  quiz:
  - q: List the 8 planets in order from the Sun.
    a: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. ('My Very Educated Mother Just Served Us Nachos')
  - q: What causes day and night?
    a: Earth rotating on its axis (one rotation = 24 hours). The side facing the Sun has day.
  - q: What causes the seasons?
    a: Earth's 23.5° axial tilt — NOT distance from the Sun. The tilt changes how directly sunlight hits each hemisphere.
  - q: What is a light year?
    a: The distance light travels in one year (~9.46 trillion km). It's a unit of DISTANCE, not time.
  - q: What is gravity?
    a: A force of attraction between any objects with mass. More mass = stronger gravity.
  - q: Why does the Moon have phases?
    a: We see different amounts of the sunlit half as the Moon orbits Earth. The Moon doesn't change shape — we just see different portions.
  - q: What is the difference between a solar and lunar eclipse?
    a: 'Solar: Moon between Earth and Sun (blocks sunlight). Lunar: Earth between Sun and Moon (Earth''s shadow on Moon).'
  - q: Why do astronauts float on the ISS?
    a: They are in continuous free-fall (orbit) around Earth. Gravity still acts — they're just falling sideways fast enough to miss Earth.
  - q: 'Common mistake: ''Seasons are caused by Earth being closer to the Sun.'' Why is this wrong?'
    a: Earth is actually slightly CLOSER to the Sun during Northern Hemisphere winter. Seasons are caused by the 23.5° tilt of Earth's axis.
  - q: What would happen if the Sun's gravity suddenly disappeared?
    a: All planets would fly off in straight lines (tangent to their orbits) at their current speed. No gravity = no orbit.
